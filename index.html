Self-Assessment Analysis

Rhetoric

My clearest growth in rhetoric came from learning to tailor communication to a specific audience rather than writing generically. In my career materials, I revised my resume to emphasize measurable outcomes over task descriptions, highlighting results like a "6x runtime improvement" and "40% reduction in error rates," language directly aligned with what technical recruiters evaluate. My cover letter shifted from a generic introduction to one that explicitly connected my background to Intuit's focus on data-driven decision-making.

In my crisis report, I applied rhetorical analysis differently: I examined how Anthropic and the U.S. Department of Defense strategically framed information during a crisis. I identified how Anthropic avoided direct confirmation of involvement while foregrounding ethical safeguards, and how the DoD maintained secrecy through strategic silence. Recognizing these patterns required me to think critically about how word choice, structure, and omission all function rhetorically.

Process

My development in process is best illustrated through the revisions I made across projects. Early in the semester, my writing tended toward description. Over time, I learned to revise with a clearer goal: making my communication more effective for its intended reader. In MP1, I revised resume bullets from task-based language to impact-driven statements with specific metrics. In MP2, I restructured my crisis report into formal sections covering executive summary, background, methods, findings, and implications. Most significantly, I moved from summarizing the crisis chronologically to analyzing the rhetorical strategies each stakeholder used. In MP3, I built training materials with a modular structure so content could be revised as the Bloomberg Terminal interface changes.

Modes and Media

In MP2, I produced both a written crisis report and a visual infographic. The infographic translated the report's core argument into a timeline, a stakeholder comparison, and a breakdown of framing strategies. It was designed for a broader, less technical audience, requiring deliberate choices about what to include and how to present complex analysis visually. In MP3, I built training materials on Notion, combining step-by-step instructions with screenshots and structured navigation. Choosing Notion itself was a medium decision: it functions more like an interactive guide than a static PDF.

Design

My growth in design came from becoming more deliberate about layout, hierarchy, and usability. In my resume, I used consistent formatting and concise bullets to make the document scannable in the seconds a recruiter typically spends on a first read. In my infographic, I used visual hierarchy to direct the reader's eye from the timeline, to stakeholder analysis, to implications, mirroring the logical structure of the written report. In my training materials, I prioritized accessibility through plain language and numbered steps so a new user could follow the guide without prior context. Across all three projects, I came to see design not as decoration but as a core part of effective communication.

Professional Work

Fraud Detection Platform

Python • Scikit-learn • Pandas • SQL • Flask/Django • March 2025 – September 2025

I built a full-stack AI-powered analytics platform for the Georgia Tech Student Government Association to detect anomalous fund disbursements across student organizations. I developed and deployed machine learning pipelines that modeled historical spending patterns and surfaced outliers and potential fraud signals in reimbursement data. The system had to be not just accurate, but interpretable and actionable for administrators who are not data scientists, demonstrating that a tool's value depends on how clearly its outputs are communicated to the people using it.
