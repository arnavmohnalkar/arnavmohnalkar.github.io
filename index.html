Self-Assessment Analysis

Rhetoric

My clearest growth in rhetoric came from learning to tailor communication to a specific audience rather than writing generically. The most concrete example is in my resume from MP1. An early draft included a bullet that read something like "worked on data pipeline optimization." After revision, that same bullet became "engineered data pipelines reducing runtime by 6x and cutting error rates by 40%." The shift from describing a task to communicating measurable impact is a rhetorical choice: it speaks directly to what a technical recruiter values. My cover letter went through a similar revision. An early draft opened with a generic summary of my background. The final version opened with a targeted sentence: "I am drawn to Intuit because it sits at the exact intersection of large-scale data infrastructure and real-world financial impact, which is precisely where I want to build my career." That sentence was written specifically for Intuit's audience, grounding my application in their values rather than my own resume.

In MP2, I applied rhetorical thinking in a different direction. Rather than crafting persuasive communication myself, I analyzed how Anthropic and the U.S. Department of Defense each framed information during a crisis. A key finding in my report was that "Anthropic's response appears designed to protect the company's reputation while avoiding direct confirmation," which I identified as a deliberate rhetorical strategy of foregrounding ethics while omitting operational specifics. Recognizing these patterns required thinking critically about how word choice, structure, and omission all function as rhetorical tools.

Process

My development in process is best illustrated through specific revision decisions I made across projects. In MP1, my initial resume bullets were task-based: they described what I did rather than what I achieved. For example, "assisted with backend development for student org platform" became "engineered a full-stack fraud detection platform serving Georgia Tech SGA, reducing disbursement anomalies flagged for manual review by 30%." After reviewing feedback and considering the audience, I rewrote every bullet to lead with outcomes and quantify impact wherever possible. That single revision pass made the document significantly more competitive for technical roles.

In MP2, my first draft of the crisis report was organized chronologically, summarizing what happened and when. After revision, I restructured it entirely around rhetorical analysis, adding formal sections for executive summary, methods, findings, and implications. This change shifted the document from a timeline into an argument, which is what the assignment actually called for. In MP3, I built the Bloomberg Terminal training materials with a modular structure from the start, specifically so individual sections could be updated without rewriting the whole guide. That was a process decision built into the design of the deliverable itself.

Modes and Media

In MP2, I produced both a written crisis report and a separate visual infographic covering the same material. The report was structured for a reader who would engage with it closely, while the infographic was designed for someone who needed a quick, visual overview. In the report I wrote: "the official communication process failed to provide clear answers, leaving journalists and experts to determine the parameters of the discussion." That sentence works in a long-form analytical document. In the infographic, that same finding became a single labeled callout under a stakeholder comparison chart, stripped of the surrounding argument. The decision about what to keep and what to cut for each format is itself a communication skill.

In MP3, I chose Notion as the platform for my Bloomberg Terminal training materials rather than a Word document or PDF. That choice was itself a media decision. Notion allowed me to create a navigable, modular guide with embedded screenshots and collapsible sections, which functions more like an interactive reference tool than a static document. A new Bloomberg user can jump directly to the section they need rather than reading linearly, which better fits how people actually use training materials.

Design

My growth in design came from becoming more deliberate about layout, hierarchy, and usability as communication choices rather than aesthetic ones. In my resume, I revised the formatting so that metrics and outcomes appear at the start of each bullet. A line like "Python, Scikit-learn, and Pandas used to develop pipelines" became "developed ML pipelines in Python and Scikit-learn, processing 3 years of disbursement data across 40+ student organizations." Leading with the action and the scale makes the information land faster for a reader scanning quickly.

In my infographic for MP2, I organized information so the reader moves from context (the timeline) to analysis (stakeholder framing) to implication, mirroring the argument structure of the written report. In my Bloomberg training materials, I prioritized accessibility through plain language and numbered steps, removing jargon like "equity derivative pricing mechanisms" and replacing it with plain descriptions of what each function does and when a student would use it. Across all three projects, design decisions were driven by asking how the reader would actually encounter and use the document.
